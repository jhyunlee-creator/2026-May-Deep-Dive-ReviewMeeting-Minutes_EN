01 Key Insights — What Does "Gender-Neutral Fantasy" Actually Mean?
1-1 "Less action" doesn't make a title gender-neutral

Trying to define gender-neutral fantasy by how much action it contains doesn't hold up. 전지적 독자 시점 / Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint has plenty of action and still draws a majority female readership, while plenty of low-action titles have completely failed to cross over. Successful gender-neutral titles like 역대급 영지 설계사 / The Ultimate Estate Designer, 나 혼자 탑에서 농사 / Solo Farming in the Tower, and 전지적 독자 시점 / Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint do tend to have fewer overt action sequences — but that's a symptom, not the cause.

"If it's a male-targeted title, but a female team member reads it and goes 'this is actually fun' — that's gender-neutral. No need to overthink it." — Heewon Shin
Platform context: The whole point of gender-neutral sourcing is to bring female readers — who are heavily concentrated in romance fantasy — into male-oriented fantasy. With the romance fantasy market softening and adult content no longer a priority, gender-neutral, thriller, and drama titles are now the main lever for growing our reader base. Data from titles like 입학용병 / Mercenary Enrollment and 킬러 베드로 / Killer Pedro backs this up: when female readers co-read a male-oriented title, retention is higher and it holds its ranking longer.
1-2 Female readers are there for the characters and story — not the fights

Male readers tend to gravitate toward good-vs-evil setups, rivalry dynamics, and physical or stat-based power progression. Female readers respond more to character relationships, psychological tension, negotiation, and exploring the world of the story. Female readers who grew up on Harry Potter-style fantasy tend to want world-building and character growth over straightforward battle arcs.

When romance readers cross into fantasy, the male lead's look and first impression are often decisive. "If it opens with the tower, the villain, monsters — I check out. But if there's a fight and the male lead looks cool, I'll keep going." (Jihyun Bae) The key to getting gender-neutral fantasy right is leading with character appeal, then letting the story unfold from there.

전지적 독자 시점 / Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint: A large female fan base already existed from the original web novel. The conflict runs on negotiation, information asymmetry, and psychological tension rather than physical combat. Female reader share sits around 25% — low in absolute terms, but well above the genre average. This is the structural pattern for gender-neutral titles: a solid male reader base with additional female reader crossover on top. The trend holds globally — in Indonesia's "Best Male Lead" vote, protagonist KDJ beat out romance leads to take first place.
1-3 Female protagonist ≠ gender-neutral / Academy titles skew male by default

Female-protagonist action titles have consistently underperformed because male readers tend to disengage. Titles launched as gender-neutral off the back of 놀이감 / The Play's success largely missed the mark. Female readers respond to character appeal and story regardless of protagonist gender; male readers, on the other hand, often drop off when the protagonist is female. Female-protagonist action titles are hard to pitch as either male-oriented or gender-neutral — female-oriented is usually the right call.

The group also aligned on academy titles: they tend to be baked with harem elements and shaped by Japanese genre conventions aimed at male readers, which makes gender-neutral classification a stretch. The only exception would be a premise that's genuinely unusual.

Don't put all your eggs in one basket: A gender-neutral strategy shouldn't hinge on a single factor (BL undertones, female protagonist, healing vibes, etc.). It needs to be a portfolio of signals spread across the title. This principle applies to how we source gender-neutral titles just as much as anything else.
02 The Character Duo — The Core Hook for Female Readers
Why it works
Strong dynamics between male characters are often a key driver of female readership and long-term engagement

The single most powerful hook for bringing in and retaining female readers in gender-neutral fantasy is the Character Duo between two male characters. Female readers tend to pick up on the dynamic between two characters, interpreting it through a BL lens — writing fan fiction, creating content, pushing it viral — which organically grows the fandom. It doesn't have to be explicit BL. The structure just needs to be shippable: it gives readers enough space to read into the relationship and project onto it. Once a dedicated female fan base forms around that dynamic, the title's spending power goes up significantly.

KDJ × Yoo Joonghyuk
Fan fiction interpreted through a BL lens blew up both domestically and internationally. Won the "Best Male Lead" vote on Webtoon Indonesia over romance leads. The clearest global example of a gender-neutral title that worked.
내가 키운 S급들 / The S-Classes That I Raised
Protagonist × Male Characters
Cute character energy plus male character dynamics that fans often read through a BL lens drive female readership. The summoned creature characters are a big part of the appeal. A case where gender-neutral structure and feminine sensibility coexist.
백작가의 망나니가 되었다 / I Became a Crazy Swordsmanship Instructor in the Game
Male Character Duo Structure
Gender-neutral readership confirmed via a compelling character duo and a shippable structure. The art style keeps the barrier to entry low.
Character Duo — Sourcing Checklist
  • Is there a secondary male character who stands on equal footing with the protagonist? (supporter, rival, companion)
  • Do the two characters go through a conflict → trust → bond arc over the course of the story?
  • Are there recurring scenes where readers can read into the pair's relationship?
  • Is the character pairing shippable — does it have the kind of viral potential that drives fan fiction and fan-made content?
03 Gender-Neutral Sourcing Criteria — Green Lights & Deal Breakers
Green Lights — Must Haves
  • Character Appeal: The protagonist needs to be visually and narratively compelling. This is the front door for romance readers crossing over.
  • Character Duo: A shippable dynamic between two male characters (see Section 02)
  • Strong Supporting Cast: The characters around the protagonist should each have their own distinct appeal
  • Story-driven Progression: Power that comes from narrative force — not physical stats
  • Fast Entry: Core premise established within the first 3 episodes, with a simple and repeatable reward loop
  • The Gut Check: A female team member reads it and says "I'd actually read this, even though it's targeted at guys"
Deal Breakers — Must Not Haves
  • Harem Structure: This includes cases where it only shows up in later chapters. Any single deal breaker disqualifies a title from gender-neutral sourcing.
  • Overly Sexualized Female Characters: In both the art and the story. Includes Japanese-style fan service.
  • Female-Protagonist Action Title: Can't be assumed gender-neutral by default. High risk of male reader drop-off.
  • Heavy Early World-Building: Leading with towers, villains, beasts, and monsters before readers have anyone to care about.
  • Academy Genre (default): Typically male-oriented through harem elements and Japanese genre conventions. Only consider an exception when the premise is genuinely unusual.
Failure Case — 회칼 하나로 아카데미를 평정했다 / Crushing the Academy with One Sashimi Knife
The early hook — a single sashimi knife as a unique item — was strong, and female readers came in initially. But harem elements and over-sexualized female character depictions (Japanese-style) showed up later, and female readers dropped off. The title did exceptionally well in Japan, which tells you it's precisely tuned to Japanese male-oriented genre conventions. This one was better off launched as male-oriented from the start.
What Good Looks Like — Gender-Neutral Titles with High Retention
킬러 베드로 / Killer Pedro, 입학용병 / Mercenary Enrollment — both confirmed that male-oriented titles with meaningful female co-readership hold their rankings longer and retain better overall. Data for 입학용병 / Mercenary Enrollment shows a high share of romance fantasy among co-read titles, which is solid evidence that female readers are there in real numbers. This is the structural argument for why gender-neutral fantasy outperforms pure male-oriented titles on long-term retention.
04 Theme Bucket Definitions
BUCKET 01 Growth / Healing Tycoon Fantasy
Definition: Fantasy where the protagonist builds and expands their own domain — a farm, an estate, a dungeon, etc. It reads like a healing or slice-of-life title on the surface, but the actual reader pleasure is tycoon mechanics: accumulating power, collecting allies, and stacking up recurring rewards. What separates this from a simple healing title is the feeling of steady, compounding growth. Given that female players make up the overwhelming majority of tycoon game audiences, we'd expect a similar pattern to emerge in webtoons.
Why it works for gender-neutral
  • Little to no action keeps the barrier to entry low
  • Female players dominate tycoon games→ That same audience behavior is likely to carry over to webtoons
  • Chemistry between the protagonist and animal/ally companions works as a variant of the Character Duo
  • Simple, repeatable reward loops encourage sustained reading without drop-off
  • "Domain expansion" gives readers a clear sense of direction and something to root for
Sourcing Checklist
  • Does Episode 1 surface the core premise (farming / estate / management) right away?
  • Is there a clear direction for expanding power or collecting allies?
  • Is the chemistry between the protagonist and their supporting cast (animals / allies) strong enough?
  • Can the story move at a good clip without getting bogged down in early world-building?
  • Does a "dark force" show up in the back half without any narrative buildup?→ Maintaining the healing tone is everything. See 김오진 case below.

BUCKET 02 Relationship-driven Fantasy
Definition: Fantasy where narrative and character relationships are the engine of the story. Action is fine, but readers keep coming back not for "the next fight" but because they need to know what happens to these characters. The Character Duo hits especially hard here, and these titles tend to build strong fandoms with high viral potential.
Why it works for gender-neutral
  • Fits the "world-building + character growth" grammar female readers know from Harry Potter and fantasy novels
  • Character Duo → fan-interpreted BL dynamics → fan fiction / viral → new female reader crossover (a genuine flywheel)
  • Negotiation, information warfare, psychological tension in place of combat → easier to get into for readers who aren't fantasy genre regulars
  • High character shippability opens up real potential for merch and event tie-ins
Sourcing Checklist
  • Is there a shippable secondary male character alongside the protagonist? (see Section 02)
  • Does the protagonist's power come from "physical stats" or "story"?→ The latter makes it much easier for female readers to get in
  • Do the characters actually go through a full trust-conflict-bond arc?
  • Does a web novel source or existing fan base already exist?→ Head start on female reader crossover. First-mover advantage is real here.

BUCKET 03 Hybrid Suspense / Thriller Name still under discussion — see notes below
Definition: Thrillers where suspense and drama take the lead over outright horror. Titles in this bucket hold tension without leaning on classic thriller tropes (ghosts, killers, jump-scare sequences). On this platform, pairing with romance or drama elements significantly raises the ceiling for mainstream success. To bring the barrier to entry down, pitching the genre as "drama" rather than "thriller" is often the smarter move.
Romance / Drama Elements
Even thrillers need romance or drama to go mainstream

Heewon Shin and Jueun Kim both agreed that, given how our platform works, thriller titles need at least a layer of romance or drama to maximize their mainstream potential. Even a thin romantic thread meaningfully increases female reader engagement. In France, thrillers without any romance consistently underperform — a female protagonist helps even further. Within the thriller space, stacking revenge, drama, melodrama, and a touch of absurdism gives titles a shot at reaching both genre fans and general readers at the same time.

Character Relationships & Reader Immersion
Relationships between characters are what actually drive thriller engagement

Jaehyun Lee and Jueun Kim both pointed to character relationships — especially ones built around a female protagonist — as the primary driver of reader immersion in thriller titles. The relationship dynamics in 니나의 마법서랍 / Nina's Magic Drawer were called out specifically as a key success factor. It's not the creatures or monsters themselves that keep readers hooked — it's the relationships characters build while facing those threats together.

What Makes Thrillers Last — Character Identity
Strong character identity is what keeps a thriller alive — monsters and protagonists alike

Jihyun Bae noted that a monster or character's distinct personality is essential for sustaining tension in a thriller. Jueun Kim added that even without creature elements, a protagonist who's genuinely off-kilter — authentically unhinged, unpredictable — can generate just as much tension. "Fake-crazy" doesn't cut it — performed quirkiness without real psychological depth falls flat. Subtle, authentic instability is what works. 스위트홈 / Sweet Home, 타인은 지옥이다 / Hell Is Other People, and 4교시 추리게임 / 4th Period Mystery Game are all strong examples of creature character identity done right.

Genre Sustainability
Thrillers are the hardest genre to hold together in the back half

Jueun Kim noted that thriller narratives frequently fall apart in later chapters — tension unravels, plausibility breaks down. The group broadly agreed that keeping thriller tension and dramatic pacing working together is the toughest challenge in the genre. That means sourcing evaluation can't just look at early-episode hooks; it has to assess whether the narrative structure can hold through the back half and whether tone is preserved through coherent plotting. School-setting thrillers in particular are prone to pacing problems from repetitive quest structures.

Pitching & Positioning — Genre Labeling
The word "thriller" is itself a barrier — we need to rethink the label

Jihyun Bae noted that 니나의 마법서랍 / Nina's Magic Drawer was able to reach beyond the existing thriller audience largely because of its cute art style and low initial barrier. Jueun Kim pointed out that "thriller" as a word carries dark, scary connotations that actively put new readers off. Kangeun Shin flagged the opportunity to pull in non-traditional reader segments, arguing that we need a broader framing — suspense rather than thriller, outright — paired with a fresh marketing angle.

This raised the question of what to call this bucket going forward. Names that came up in discussion:

Suspense Drama Dark Drama Noir Drama Atmospheric Thriller Drama Suspense Soft Thriller

→ Next step: Consider flexible genre tagging — thriller → drama. Lead with drama framing, let the thriller element reveal itself as the series develops. Bring a specific positioning pitch to BU.

Variety vs. Metrics-driven Sourcing
Keeping genre depth for the core fanbase matters too

Jihyun Bae pushed back on the idea of only chasing metrics — thriller fans are a real, committed audience and they need to be served with diverse titles. Thrillers keep getting deprioritized, but the spending power of hardcore thriller readers is measurable and real. Jueun Kim proposed a dual-purpose sourcing structure: find the common ground that speaks to existing readers, while leaving room for intentional curation beyond what the data alone would suggest.

Genre Distinction
Suspense and thriller aren't the same — and their success factors aren't either
Suspense
Tension from the audience knowing something the protagonist doesn't — dread through dramatic irony
  • Information asymmetry is the core device
  • Requires a carefully constructed setup to land
  • Readers watch danger approaching while the protagonist remains unaware
  • 니나의 마법서랍 / Nina's Magic Drawer, 모닥불 친구들 / Bonfire Friends, etc.
Thriller
"You never know what this protagonist is going to do next" — tension through character unpredictability
  • The protagonist's unpredictable behavior is the core device
  • Authentic instability, not performed quirkiness — the difference matters enormously
  • Readers experience tension by following the protagonist through it
  • 소녀재판 / The Girl's Revenge, 쪽팔려 게임 / The Embarrassing Game, 살인스타그램 / Kill Stagram, etc.
Genre Labeling Strategy — How to Position This as Hybrid

Core principle: The word "thriller" primes readers for something dark and scary — and that's a barrier. Especially when we're trying to reach readers beyond the existing thriller base, flexibly pitching the genre as drama or fantasy first is the smarter play.

What to do: Consider re-tagging thriller → drama / Open with a drama frame, let it transition into thriller as the series develops / Build a specific positioning pitch for BU and advocate for it / If a thumbnail is too scary, flag it for a potential swap (Thailand precedent: a thumbnail change directly improved performance)

05 DDR Review Summary by Title
나 혼자 탑에서 농사 / Solo Farming in the Tower → Bucket 01: Growth / Healing Tycoon Fantasy

Looks like a healing/slice-of-life title on the surface, but the actual entertainment structure is tycoon. The "tower + farming" premise is right there from Episode 1, and the chemistry between the protagonist and his rabbit companions is strong. An appealing supporting character — the quest manager — shows up early, and the reward loop is clean and simple. The animal-protagonist chemistry (similar to the Ppodungi × Lloyd dynamic in 역대급 영지 설계사 / The Ultimate Estate Designer) is a clear driver of female reader immersion. Has held a spot in TW UB Top 30 consistently — well ahead of 김오진과 이상한 동물들 / Kim Ojin and the Strange Animals and 그리핀 / Griffin.

Classification: Tycoon Fantasy, not healing/slice-of-life. This becomes the benchmark for sourcing similar premises going forward (farming, estate management, dungeon management, etc.). The fact that it keeps adding new world and relationship threads in later chapters is structural evidence for its long-term retention potential.
전지적 독자 시점 / Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint → Bucket 02: Relationship-driven Fantasy

This title's gender-neutral nature isn't about less action — it's the Character Duo (KDJ × YJH). A large female fan base already existed from the original web novel, and fan fiction — widely interpreted through a BL lens — went viral domestically and internationally at the same time. Despite having real action, the narrative structure — where the protagonist's power comes from the story itself rather than physical strength — keeps the barrier low for female readers. Female reader share is around 25%, which confirms the structural pattern for gender-neutral titles: a solid male reader base with meaningful female reader crossover on top. The group placed it in the Relationship-driven Fantasy bucket.

Classification: The definitive global case for gender-neutral Relationship-driven Fantasy. The benchmark criteria for sourcing similar titles are "strong male character duo + story-driven growth." To be used as a reference when we go after popular web novel adaptations.
니나의 마법서랍 / Nina's Magic Drawer → Bucket 03: Hybrid Suspense / Thriller

The title that generated the most genre classification debate. The core question was whether it's a fantasy-infused thriller or a gender-neutral fantasy. The group landed on: "a thriller, but one that doesn't feel like a thriller." The cute art style and bright color palette strip away the usual barrier to thriller entry. The 80% female readership comes from the art style, the novelty of the premise, and the cushioning effect of the fantasy mechanics (the magic drawer) — not from the thriller genre itself. Character relationship dynamics are the primary driver of immersion, and its real value is in demonstrating a structure that brought in new readers rather than converting existing thriller fans.

Classification: Hybrid Suspense / Thriller. Can be approached as drama rather than thriller for genre tagging. For similar titles in sourcing, the criteria are: "cute or bright visuals + fantasy mechanics + low horror intensity + strong character relationships."

Conclusion

This review confirmed that what makes gender-neutral Non-Action Fantasy work has nothing to do with how much action there is — it comes down to Relationships, Progression, Expansion, and Character Appeal. Of those, the Character Duo is the single most powerful hook for bringing in and keeping female readers, and it needs to be the top item on the sourcing checklist. The Suspense/Thriller bucket can absolutely win a gender-neutral audience when it's positioned as a "thriller that doesn't feel like a thriller" — one that leads with drama, romance, and strong character identity. The final bucket name is still to be determined. Going forward, sourcing will be organized around the three Theme Buckets below rather than genre classification alone.
